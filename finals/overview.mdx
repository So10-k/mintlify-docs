The Grand Final closes out Season 1 of Mia's Quiz Tournament. Eleven players entered the tournament; over eight weeks of bracket battles and a few rounds of redemption-bracket comebacks, four remain. The Grand Final crowns the season's champion in three back-to-back matches.

</Note>

## The three matches of finals night

<Steps>
  <Step title="🥈 Losers' Bracket Final · Grandpa vs Sam">
    Fifteen multiple-choice questions, thirty seconds each, most-correct wins. **Famous places** trivia at extreme difficulty. Winner advances to the championship; runner-up takes home third place and a standing ovation.
  </Step>
  <Step title="🏆 Winners' Bracket Final · Karen vs Marc">
    Same fifteen-question / thirty-second format. A **separate** set of famous-places trivia at the same extreme difficulty — none of the questions overlap with the losers' final. Winner advances.
  </Step>
  <Step title="👑 Championship · WB winner vs LB winner">
    Eight harder, **mixed-topic** questions across geography, science, history, literature, art, sports, mythology — every question on a different subject so no one can study a single area. First to five wins. Sudden-death tiebreaker if the eighth question ends in a tie.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Note>
  The championship's question set is intentionally kept secret from the host (Sam might be playing in it). The questions are generated by AI on a mystery-misc topic and never shown to him in the editor or the host HUD until the moment they air.
</Note>

## The prize

The first-ever **Mia's Quiz Tournament champion** title — and bragging rights for an entire family group chat. There is no cash prize, no trophy that anyone can hold, and no parade. There is, however, an [official theme song](https://quiz.miaswebsites.art/listen) and a closing-credits slide with your name on it.

## Why the format is what it is

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Why a double-elimination bracket?">
    With eleven players, a single-elimination bracket eliminates someone in week one and they sit on the bench for months. Double-elimination gives every player two chances — lose in the winners' side and you drop to the losers' side, where you can climb back up to the final. The two finals exist because both brackets need a champion before the crown match.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Why famous places for the bracket finals?">
    Sam picked the topic. It's broadcastable, fits the picture-book vibe, scales from "easy" (Eiffel Tower) to "extreme" (lesser-known UNESCO sites, specific architects, geological records) without being obscure for obscurity's sake.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Why misc for the championship?">
    A single-topic championship lets one player out-study the other. A misc round — every question on a different subject — rewards general knowledge over cramming. It's also intentionally hidden from Sam since he's a possible finalist.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Why three matches instead of one?">
    Because both bracket finals matter to the people in them. Compressing the night into one championship would mean Karen, Marc, Grandpa, and Sam each get fewer minutes on camera. Three matches gives every finalist their own beat.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
